What is Demos?

Start here. A plain-English introduction to the Demos network, DACS, and why DNO observes the Demos testnet from the outside.

In plain English: Demos is a blockchain project trying to connect things that normally can’t easily talk to each other — different blockchains, websites, identity records, and AI agents — so they can exchange information and value in a way others can verify.

The short version

Most digital systems live in separate worlds. A company might have one identity in a government register, another on a blockchain, and accounts on several Web2 platforms. A piece of software acting on its behalf — an agent — may need to prove who it represents, check another party’s claims, agree on terms, pay across different chains, and later prove what happened.

Demos describes itself as a layer-1 blockchain built to act as a connecting layer for exactly this kind of cross-system work, rather than as another chain competing to replace the others. Its authors call the broader connected environment the “Omniweb.” Demos is currently described as a testnet, with a mainnet launch presented by the project as a planned future step.

Why this matters

Most existing tools solve only one piece of that problem — identity, or payments, or data lookups — but not the whole chain of steps end to end. Demos is designed around the broader problem: how different digital contexts can verify each other, coordinate, settle value, and leave a record, without everyone having to trust one central company or platform.

A simple example

Imagine one software agent wants to buy a service from another. Before paying, it might need to know:

  • Who is the other agent, and who does it represent?
  • Is the company behind it real — can that be checked against an authority?
  • What exactly are both sides agreeing to?
  • Can payment happen even if the two sides are on different blockchains?
  • Can both sides prove later what was agreed and what happened?

Demos and DACS are aimed at this kind of problem: verifiable interaction between agents, identities, Web2 sources, and blockchains.

How Demos describes its technology

Per the Demos yellowpaper, Demos is a layer-1 blockchain with a consensus design of its own and a focus on interoperability across Web2, Web3, and multiple chains. For a newcomer, the most useful way in is the small set of building blocks the project highlights — described here by what they do, with the project’s own name for each:

One identity across many systems
A way for a single identity to work across multiple blockchains and Web2 platforms at once. Demos calls this Cross-Context Identity (CCI); described as testnet-stage.
Verifying web data on-chain
A way for network validators to fetch data from a normal website or API and record a verifiable, on-chain attestation of what was seen. Demos calls this DAHR (Data Agnostic HTTP(S) Relay); described as testnet-stage.
Private coordination, public proof
Private channels where only the participants see the details, while the fact that an agreement happened is anchored publicly. Demos calls this L2PS (Layer-2 Privacy Subnets); described as testnet-stage.
Settling across chains
Mechanisms intended to let value move between different blockchains in a coordinated, all-or-nothing way. Demos describes Liquidity Tanks and native bridges for this; described as testnet-stage with partial chain coverage.
Proving facts without oversharing
Methods that let someone prove a specific fact from a website without revealing all of the underlying data. Composed open standards (TLSNotary, zkTLS) used in the DACS vetting stage.

What is DACS?

DACS (Demos Agent Commerce Standards) is an open standard, published by KyneSys Labs, for commerce between software agents. A software agent is a program that can act for a person, company, or system. DACS breaks an agent-to-agent transaction into five stages — in plain terms, it answers: Who are you? Can I check that? What are we agreeing to? How do we pay? How do we prove what happened?

1 · Identify
Each party presents a verifiable identity and what it is offering.
2 · Vet
Each party checks the other’s claims against authoritative sources — for example, confirming that a Legal Entity Identifier exists in the public GLEIF register — where possible without revealing more than needed.
3 · Negotiate
The parties agree on terms, with support for private negotiation and a signed agreement.
4 · Settle
Value or deliverables are exchanged, where possible atomically across different chains.
5 · Verify
The whole session is anchored as a tamper-evident audit record that can be checked later.

DACS is designed to work with existing open standards (such as W3C Verifiable Credentials, ERC-8004, AP2, and x402) rather than replace them. As published, it is an early specification (a v0.1 baseline, with some per-stage standards at v0.2), maintained under a single steward.

What is live, and what is roadmap?

Early blockchain projects often mix working testnet components, experimental features, and future plans in the same public narrative. When reading anything about Demos, it helps to separate three things:

  • Observed state — what an independent observer like DNO can currently see from the public testnet.
  • Project claims — what Demos or KyneSys Labs describe in their own materials.
  • Roadmap — features the project describes as planned, in progress, or not yet active.

As of June 2026, Demos describes the network as running on testnet, with its core primitives reported as operational at the testnet stage, and a mainnet launch described as a planned future step.

Why DNO observes Demos

DNO watches the Demos testnet from the outside and publishes assessments of network health based only on what it can directly observe. As new infrastructure for agent-to-agent coordination develops, an independent, non-participating observer becomes more useful — precisely because it has no stake in what it reports. DNO does not run the network, recommend validators, score or rank participants, certify anyone, or take part in any transaction.
